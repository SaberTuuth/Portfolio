Cyber Breach

About Game of Life

Cyber Breach is a movement-based precision FPS set in a steampunk, futuristic world. Built in Unity by a team of five at Full Sail University, the game challenges players to eliminate weak-point-driven robot enemies and infiltrate enemy systems. Inspired by Doom and Ghostrunner, it combines fast-paced action with puzzle-like combat that rewards timing and accuracy.

Project Information

Genre: FPS | Patformer
Role: Gameplay Developer
Team-Size: 5
Development Time: 2 Months
software/language: Visual Studios Github C# Unity

Introduction

Throughout development, I collaborated closely with my team to align technical implementation with our design goals. During early planning, we aimed to give the player five distinct knife types each with unique effects but due to time constraints, I prioritized three: regular, frost, and fire. This required careful planning and modular code structure to allow rapid iteration and testing. I also contributed to team discussions around AI behavior and level pacing, ensuring that our combat system felt responsive and balanced while remaining visually engaging and mechanically clear.

Projectile Knife System

As part of my key contributions to the project, I developed a modular projectile system to support three distinct player weapons, each with unique gameplay mechanics and visual feedback. For the Regular Knife, I implemented realistic physics-based movement using rigidbody components, allowing the knife to arc naturally with gravity. Upon impact, I added visual feedback by triggering a coroutine that temporarily changed the color of the hit target, enhancing player responsiveness. The Frost Knife introduced crowd control functionality by leveraging Unity’s NavMeshAgent.isStopped property to freeze enemy AI on contact. I reinforced this effect with a custom freezing visual, achieved by overlaying a translucent, scaled duplicate of the enemy mesh to simulate an ice-encased appearance. The Fire Knife brought a damage-over-time mechanic into the system, where I used a coroutine with yield return new WaitForSeconds to apply periodic damage ticks, simulating a burning effect over several seconds. This modular approach allowed for easy expansion and maintenance while supporting varied gameplay experiences for each weapon type.

Level Obstacles

For the level design and gameplay challenges, I created a variety of interactive, platform-based obstacles that tested the player’s movement, timing, and spatial awareness. This included the implementation of disappearing platforms that required precise jumps and quick decision-making, enhancing the pacing and tension of the level. I also integrated pressure plates that activated or deactivated specific elements in the environment—such as unlocking doors, triggering hazards, or toggling platform visibility—adding puzzle-solving elements to the platforming experience. Additionally, I developed level triggers to manage in-game events like enemy spawns, checkpoint activations, or environmental changes, ensuring smooth transitions and responsive feedback tied directly to the player's actions. These components worked together to create dynamic, engaging gameplay that blended traditional level design with interactive, game-driven mechanics.

Additional Contributions

In addition to my primary responsibilities, I actively supported my four team members throughout development by assisting with debugging various gameplay systems, including AI behavior, core game mechanics, and UI functionality. I helped identify and resolve logic errors, optimize component interactions, and ensure smoother integration across systems, fostering a more stable and polished game experience. I also implemented music transition triggers that responded to in-game events such as entering combat zones or reaching key narrative moments. These audio transitions enhanced the overall atmosphere and emotional pacing of the game, contributing to a more immersive and dynamic player experience.

What I Learned

This project taught me the importance of balancing ambition with scope, especially when working under time constraints in a team environment. I gained hands-on experience with modular programming practices, which allowed me to iterate quickly and adapt to design changes. I also deepened my understanding of Unity’s physics system, NavMesh AI controls, and coroutine-based timing for gameplay effects. Most importantly, I learned how to collaborate effectively with a multidisciplinary team, communicate technical ideas clearly, and troubleshoot complex interactions between gameplay systems and visual feedback.
